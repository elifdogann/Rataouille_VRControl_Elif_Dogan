This project ultimately developed into more than a VR parkour prototype. Across its iterations, it became a structured interaction system in which locomotion, embodiment, indirect manipulation, and environmental state transitions were designed as connected layers rather than isolated mechanics. The final implementation combined a gesture-supported controller scheme, a visible rat-human control metaphor, themed environmental rewards, and a tool-based puzzle sequence into one gameplay flow. In that sense, the project moved away from conventional VR movement design and toward a mediated control model in which player intent is expressed through controller motion, interpreted through a layered locomotion architecture, and resolved through interaction outcomes in the environment.

A defining technical achievement of the project was the shift from raw or overlapping control logic to clearer architectural separation. The locomotion system evolved from multiple scripts directly affecting movement into a more stable structure with distinct responsibilities: input abstraction, movement logic, and visual representation. In the locomotion documentation, this separation is described explicitly as a three-layer architecture built around ControllerInputReader, ParkourMovementController, and VisualRope, where anchors remain spatial references rather than control owners. That separation was important not only for debugging, but also for preserving the project’s core identity: the player does not simply press a button to move, but activates movement through gesture-linked controller input that is interpreted into motion.

The final controller mapping reinforced this identity by formalizing asymmetric roles for the two hands. Steering was tied to left-controller index trigger plus lateral tilt, forward locomotion to right-controller index trigger plus forward extension, and jumping to a downward swing while both index triggers were pressed. In parallel, the knife became the entry point to object interaction, and the same controller logic extended into indirect manipulation: the player first acquires the knife, then uses it to approach and carry the key, align it with the chest slot, and trigger a chest-to-book transition. This made the entire experience feel like one continuous system rather than a series of unrelated gameplay modes.

Another important outcome was the decision to prioritize readability and reliability over uncontrolled physical complexity. The project experimented with more physics-heavy attachment ideas, but the final design settled on snap-assisted key transport and transform-based chest validation using position and rotation tolerances. Once the key reached the correct alignment, the chest disappeared, the knife could be cleared, and a book appeared as the state-change result, supported by audio and optional particle feedback. Together with the BoxActivator-based start sequence that gradually revealed the gameplay objects after an intentional start action, this created a complete runtime loop with a controlled beginning, a readable interaction arc, and a clear final reward state.

In its final form, the project can therefore be understood as a mediated VR gameplay prototype built around three interconnected ideas: gesture-driven locomotion, proxy-based embodiment, and tool-based indirect interaction. What makes the project distinct is not only that it avoids standard joystick-centered movement, but that it treats movement, perspective, and interaction as parts of the same design language. Rather than presenting a player who simply controls an avatar directly, the project constructs an experience in which influence is staged, visible, and mechanically meaningful—from controller gesture, to body motion, to puzzle completion, and finally to scene transformation.
